WiFi necessary hardware


Necessary Hardware

• Integrated WNIC into notebooks are not the ideal solution
Integrated WNIC (Wireless Network Interface Controller) that enables Wi-Fi communications in the notebook chassis are not the ideal solution for penetration testers as they can not provide the necessary signal power and receiving sensitivity, crucial parameters for a successful wireless attack.

• external USB Wi-Fi dongle are better
A better option is to buy an external USB Wi-Fi dongle. This is especially true if you plan to perform your attacks from a virtualized environment as an integrated wireless card will simply work as an Ethernet card in your guest OS
What to look when we buying a new Wi-Fi adapter
▪ Signal power
▪ Receiver sensitivity
▪ Linux driver support

Antenna of Wi-Fi adapters (APs)
external USB Wi-Fi dongle with integrated antenna are not the best choice when it comes to penetration testing. We need a flexible adapter that gives us the ability to change the attached antenna to fit your scenario.
• Power Gain (dB/dBi/dBd):
Power Gain is the connection range of a Wi-Fi adapter and is specified using decibels(dB):
◇ dBi
◇ dBd (1 dBd = 2.14dBi)
◇ dBm (measured in mW not dB) → measures the power the antenna is emitting, which can play a significant role in how much range the router has
• Types of antennas:
◇ Omnidirectional
They are designed to pick up signals coming from multiple directions , typically in a 360° area. Because of that, they are commonly found on all wireless enabled consumer devices.
Power Gain ranges from 2 dBi to 9 dBi. But because power spreads in each direction, their power gain is inherently lower than directional antennas which focus their energy across a narrow angle.
◇ Directional
They their power gain is inherently higher than omnidirectional antennas, because focus their energy across a narrow angle.
Power Gain of these antennas can be easily over 12 dBi
